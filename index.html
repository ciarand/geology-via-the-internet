a brief history of life and the oceans

Fossils from Japan

Archean (4,000 Mya to 2,500 Mya)

  • The Earth would not be recognizable to us today. The atmosphere was a toxic mix of gases, including methane and ammonia.

  • First life appears somewhere in this period. They mostly just hang out and absorb ambient (heat) energy.

  • The planet is still extremely volatile, with volcanoes (see pic) found much more commonly than today.

  • Toward the end of this period the early life begins to evolve to take advantage of the ambient energy provided by the sun.

Proterozoic (2,500 Mya to 542 Mya)

  • No predators.

  • Also, most of the creatures in the (Oxygenated, not as salty) Ocean are pretty passive things.

  • Lots of nonvertebrates.

  • Most popular feature: ability to face toward incoming food stream.

Phanerozoic (542 Mya to present)

Paleozoic (542 Mya to 251 Mya)

Cambrian (542 Mya to 488 Mya)

  • At the beginning of this period Rodina is in the middle of breaking into multiple continents

  • First real explosion of "complex life" - unimaginatively called the "Cambrian Explosion"

  • Brachiopods, archeocyathids, small shelly fossils and other invertebrates evolved and thrived.

  • Pretty mild climate, no real glaciation

Ordivician (488 Mya to 447 Mya)

  • My favorite period name

  • Trilobites, blastoids, corals, crinoids, bryozoans, and other fun-to-pronounce animals first appear here

  • Inland seas all over (at least for the beginning), so marine invertebrates are the biggest and baddest life out there.

  • Some evidence of terrestrial colonization

  • During the later part of this period the Appalachian mountains were forming, causing the inland seas to regress

Silurian (447 Mya to 416 Mya)

  • While the Ordivician was kind of a wild time for the climate, this period represents a relative stabilization. The glaciers that had accumulated during the last period melt during this period, raising the sea levels once again.

  • Fish with jaws becomes mainstream

  • Freshwater fish are now also a thing

  • Compelling and widespread evidence of land colonization

Devonian (416 Mya to 359 Mya)

  • Brachipods like tabulate and rugose corals were mainstream, which meant building huge coral reefs was too.

  • Fish suddenly become super diverse. Jawless fish, jawed fish, you name the type of jaw and there are fish that have it.

  • The 2 huge continents (Gondwana and "Euramerica") line up, ready to crash into each other during the next period.

Mississippian (359 Mya to 318 Mya)

  • The 2 continents are heading toward each other, slowly reducing the amount of coastline

  • This decreases the diversity of local shallow-water marine animals

Pennsylvanian (318 Mya to 299 Mya)

  • Many animals evolve themselves a reproductive system that uses an amniotic egg, as it provides many benefits for terrestrial creatures.

  • Multiple transgressions and regressions.

Permian (299 Mya to 251 Mya)

  • The continents have merged together into Pangea

  • Massive unified Ocean with a single supercontinent in the middle

  • Largest mass extinction event in all (geologically) recorded history happens at the end of this period, killing off most of the marine invertebrates we’ve come to know and love

Mesozoic (251 Mya to 65.5 Mya)

Triassic (251 Mya to 200 Mya)

  • This period starts in the aftermath of a large extinction event, which wiped out much of the diversity that had been building up until that point.

  • Pangea is still together at the beginning of this period, but begins to separate about halfway through.

  • Just as life’s increasing in diversity there’s another extinction event at the end of this period.

Jurassic (200 Mya to 145 Mya)

  • The "Age of Reptiles"

  • Pangea has started splitting into Gondwana (North) and Laurasia (South)

  • Splitting the continent removes some of the landlocking, making the climate much more humid

  • This new lush environment was perfect for the dinosaurs and other reptiles, and they thrived as a result.

Cretaceous (145 Mya to 65 Mya)

  • The climate pretty temperate, so the sea levels are high all around. This creates a number of inland seas on the continents.

  • Flowering plants (Angiosperms) hit the scene.

  • Dinosaurs continue to dominate throught his period, though early mammals and birds start to gain a foothold.

  • The breaking apart of Pangea has changed the Ocean currents. That, combined with the higher sea levels means that calcium carbonate (chalk - dead plankton) gets spread all over both the Ocean floor and the continents.

  • Massive extinction event, probably from a large space rock filled with space chemicals. These space chemicals (Iridium) got spread all over the planet’s rock record, showing us how significant this impact was.

Cenozoic (65.5 Mya to present)

Paleogene (65.5 Mya to 23 Mya)

  • Huge mammals like elephants begin to roam the Earth

  • First voles and hamsters

  • Worldwide marine regression as the Earth cools (not good for our marine friends!)

Neogene (23 Mya to 1.8 Mya)

  • Mountain building everywhere: Sierra Nevada, Cascades, and the Andes

  • Huge explosion of cool new animal forms, like fast horses and large predators

  • The Panama land bridge appears

  • Climate begins to cool about halfway through this period

  • The Himalayas are built

Quaternary (1.8 Mya to present)

  • Up through and during the Quaternary (about 3 Mya to the present) the Earth’s surface was cooling, creating a series of ice ages.
